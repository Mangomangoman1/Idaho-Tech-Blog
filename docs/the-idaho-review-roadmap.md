# The Idaho Review Roadmap

> **Purpose:** Define what The Idaho Review is trying to become, why it should exist, what it owes Idaho readers, and how it becomes the definitive technology resource for Idaho businesses, public leaders, students, workers, and ordinary residents.
>
> **Working brand:** The Idaho Review  
> **Core line:** Idaho’s technology economy, reported and mapped.  
> **Operating ambition:** Become the place Idaho looks when it needs to understand what technology is doing, what to build next, what to study, what to fund, what to fix, and what to avoid.

---

## 1. The Claim

The Idaho Review exists to answer one practical question:

**What should Idaho do with technology now?**

That question has different forms depending on who is asking.

- A business owner asks: **What should we implement next, and what is a waste of money?**
- A founder asks: **Where is there a real Idaho problem worth building around?**
- A politician asks: **What technology issues should I understand before I promise anything?**
- A student asks: **What should I study if I want a future in Idaho?**
- A worker asks: **Which skills will still matter five years from now?**
- A farmer asks: **Which tools help with water, labor, repair, and cost — and which ones only add another subscription?**
- A city or county asks: **What infrastructure, software, data, and staffing choices will matter next?**
- An ordinary Idaho resident asks: **What is going on in technology, and how does it touch my town, job, school, utility bill, clinic, shop, farm, or home?**

The Idaho Review should have serious answers.

Not slogans. Not borrowed Silicon Valley narratives. Not press-release rewrites. Not generic articles about innovation. Serious answers that name the company, the public system, the constraint, the opportunity, the cost, the tradeoff, the source, and the next decision.

If we do this correctly, The Idaho Review becomes more than a publication. It becomes a working map of Idaho’s future.

## 2. The North Star

**The Idaho Review will become Idaho’s practical intelligence layer for technology, business, infrastructure, and workforce decisions.**

That means we are not only writing about technology. We are building the resource layer that helps Idaho act wisely around technology.

The test is simple:

> If someone in Idaho has to make a technology-related decision, The Idaho Review should help them make it better.

That decision might be small:

- Which AI tool should my office try first?
- Should I replace this software or fix the process around it?
- Is fiber coming to my town?
- Is this company real?
- What does Micron’s expansion mean for nearby businesses?

Or it might be large:

- What should Idaho fund?
- What should Idaho teach?
- What should Idaho protect?
- Where are the state’s technical bottlenecks?
- Which industries are quietly becoming technology industries?
- What will happen if power, water, broadband, labor, or repair capacity cannot keep up?

The Idaho Review should become the place where these questions are collected, clarified, researched, and answered.

## 3. The Psyche

The Idaho Review should think like an operator, write like a regional publication, and behave like a public utility for decision-making.

### We think like an operator

We care about what works.

Technology is not real because it was announced. It becomes real when somebody has to install it, pay for it, train staff on it, maintain it, insure it, repair it, explain it to a customer, or defend it in a public meeting.

That is the operating layer we care about.

### We write like a regional publication

We are locally literate. We know Idaho is not one market. Boise is not Idaho Falls. Magic Valley is not the Wood River Valley. North Idaho is not the Treasure Valley. Rural Idaho is not just a smaller version of the city.

We do not flatten the state into one tech narrative.

### We behave like a public utility for decisions

A useful resource should be findable, clear, current, and honest about uncertainty.

When we do not know, we say so. When a claim needs verification, we mark it. When a company provided the claim, we label it. When a public record supports the claim, we link it. When the evidence is weak, we do not pretend it is strong.

Credibility is the asset.

## 4. The Audience We Serve

The Idaho Review has several audiences. The goal is not to dilute the publication for all of them. The goal is to build a resource deep enough that each group can use it from their own position.

### 1. Idaho business owners

They need clear guidance on what technology is worth adopting.

They do not need hype. They need practical judgment:

- What should we automate?
- What should stay human?
- What exposes us to risk?
- What can we do with our current staff?
- What will this cost after the first month?
- What breaks if the vendor disappears?
- What are other Idaho businesses doing?

### 2. Idaho founders and builders

They need a sharper map of local problems.

The best companies are often built around constraints others ignore:

- Water measurement
- Rural repair access
- Equipment downtime
- Public-sector software gaps
- Workforce scheduling
- Broadband reliability
- Farm data ownership
- Healthcare administration
- Small-business operations
- Energy project coordination

The Idaho Review should help builders find real problems, not fashionable ones.

### 3. Public officials and candidates

They need a technology agenda grounded in Idaho reality.

If a politician asks, “What should I run on?” the answer should not be “support innovation.” That means nothing.

Useful answers look like this:

- Make rural broadband accountability measurable.
- Build technical career paths that do not require leaving the state.
- Treat water data, energy planning, and permitting software as infrastructure.
- Support right-to-repair access for agriculture and small businesses.
- Modernize public systems without handing every process to opaque vendors.
- Help small businesses adopt safe AI and automation without exposing customer data.
- Build cybersecurity capacity for counties, schools, clinics, utilities, and small firms.

The Idaho Review should be able to publish that agenda with evidence.

### 4. Students and workers

They need to know what to study and why it matters here.

The answer cannot be only “learn to code.” Idaho needs hybrid workers:

- electricians who understand networks
- farm workers who understand sensors and irrigation systems
- bookkeepers who understand automation
- welders who understand robotics
- IT staff who understand cybersecurity
- nurses who understand digital workflows
- mechanics who understand diagnostics
- public employees who understand data systems
- small-business operators who understand AI limits

The Idaho Review should explain the real skill map.

### 5. Ordinary Idaho residents

They need technology coverage that connects to daily life.

Technology is not only apps, funding rounds, or national policy. It is also:

- whether the clinic portal works
- whether a school can maintain student devices
- whether a farm can repair equipment before weather changes
- whether a county system can process permits
- whether a business loses sales when the router fails
- whether emergency communication works during fire season
- whether power and water planning can handle growth

The Idaho Review should make invisible systems visible.

## 5. The Values

### 1. Idaho reality before imported narrative

We do not force Idaho into someone else’s technology story.

If a national trend matters here, we explain the Idaho version: the towns, industries, constraints, public systems, labor markets, geography, and costs that change the story.

### 2. Specific evidence before smooth prose

A source-linked fact is worth more than a polished paragraph.

Good work names:

- the company
- the town
- the system
- the public agency
- the document
- the person responsible
- the budget
- the deadline
- the failure mode
- the next decision

### 3. Practical usefulness before attention

We are not chasing outrage or novelty.

A piece is successful if it helps someone make a better decision, ask a better question, avoid a bad purchase, find a useful program, understand a constraint, or see an opportunity.

### 4. Skepticism without cynicism

We are skeptical because technology decisions have costs.

We are not cynical. We want Idaho to build better systems. But we do not treat every announcement as progress.

### 5. Local respect without boosterism

We can be proud of Idaho without writing chamber-of-commerce copy.

Respect means telling the truth about strengths and bottlenecks.

### 6. Operators over spectators

The people closest to the work matter.

A broadband installer, county clerk, farm mechanic, IT manager, water operator, school technician, or small-business office manager may understand the practical issue better than the keynote speaker.

### 7. Maintenance is part of technology

Launches are easy to cover. Maintenance is where reality shows up.

We cover repair, staffing, training, vendor lock-in, legacy systems, replacement costs, downtime, documentation, and the person who has to restart the system when it fails.

### 8. Open uncertainty

If something is unknown, we say it is unknown.

If a claim is based on a company statement, we say that. If a number is from a public filing, we link it. If a fact needs verification, we mark it instead of laundering it into certainty.

## 6. The Editorial Doctrine

The Idaho Review should organize coverage around questions Idaho actually needs answered.

### The five-question test

Before publishing a serious piece, ask:

1. What is the Idaho object?  
   A company, system, place, institution, person, project, public decision, infrastructure asset, workflow, or constraint.

2. What changed?  
   A project launched, funding moved, a tool entered a workflow, a system failed, a policy changed, a market shifted, or a job path opened.

3. Why does Idaho change the story?  
   Geography, water, power, agriculture, broadband, housing, labor, public land, local government, distance, trust, or small-business margins.

4. Who can verify it?  
   Public records, source links, interviews, meeting packets, filings, datasets, budgets, contracts, job postings, permits, or direct observation.

5. What happens next?  
   A vote, buildout, deadline, hiring push, budget decision, implementation risk, or metric to watch.

If a piece cannot answer at least three of these, it is not ready to be an article. It may be a pitch, research note, field-note request, or watchlist item.

### The anti-doctrine

We do not publish:

- generic AI explainers with no Idaho object
- startup puff pieces with no operating detail
- press releases rewritten as reporting
- “innovation community” articles that name no actual companies, schools, agencies, funders, customers, or workers
- listicles that do not help a decision
- policy takes without documents or local stakes
- business advice that ignores cost, staff time, privacy, or maintenance
- technology optimism that does not mention failure modes

## 7. The Core Products

The Idaho Review should become a set of durable editorial products. Articles matter, but products compound.

### 1. Idaho Tech Map

A living directory of Idaho’s technology economy.

It should include:

- companies
- labs
- universities
- broadband providers
- public programs
- workforce programs
- startup support organizations
- manufacturers
- agtech companies
- energy organizations
- repair and hardware resources
- AI and automation service providers
- cybersecurity firms
- public-sector technology initiatives

Each entry should eventually contain:

- name
- location
- category
- what they do
- why they matter in Idaho
- source links
- last updated date
- leadership/contact where appropriate
- employment/career links
- public contracts or funding where available
- related stories
- reporting status: unverified, source-linked, interviewed, profiled

The map is the utility layer. It makes the site useful before the archive is large.

### 2. Idaho Tech Briefing

A recurring briefing on what changed and what to watch.

Default structure:

- one company or market signal
- one infrastructure signal
- one public-sector or policy signal
- one workforce signal
- one practical business technology signal
- one open question

The briefing should become the rhythm of the publication.

### 3. The Constraint

A series about the limits shaping Idaho’s next decade.

Core constraints:

- water
- power
- broadband
- labor
- housing
- repair access
- permitting capacity
- public-sector software
- cybersecurity readiness
- data quality
- capital
- trust
- distance

This series gives the publication a serious point of view: Idaho’s future is not only about ambition. It is about operating conditions.

### 4. Idaho Business Technology Guides

Practical guidance for Idaho businesses.

These should answer questions like:

- What should a small business automate first?
- What should stay human?
- How should a business evaluate AI tools?
- What data should never be pasted into a chatbot?
- How do you choose a point-of-sale, scheduling, CRM, or accounting workflow?
- When is custom software worth it?
- When is a spreadsheet still fine?
- How should a business prepare for broadband outages?
- What cybersecurity basics matter for a five-person firm?

These guides create direct value for business readers.

### 5. Idaho Skill Map

A student and workforce product.

It should connect Idaho industries to practical skills:

- semiconductors → process technicians, cleanroom operations, materials, electrical, mechanical, data, safety
- energy/nuclear → welding, engineering, cybersecurity, operations, instrumentation, compliance
- agriculture → irrigation technology, mechanics, data, sensors, water management, food safety
- broadband → fiber splicing, networking, tower work, mapping, customer support
- healthcare → IT support, cybersecurity, billing systems, data privacy, workflow automation
- manufacturing → controls, robotics, PLCs, CAD, quality systems, maintenance
- public sector → GIS, records systems, cybersecurity, procurement, data management
- repair/hardware → diagnostics, microsoldering, board-level work, parts logistics, customer trust

The skill map should answer the student question: **What should I study if I want a future in Idaho?**

### 6. Public Technology Agenda

A nonpartisan policy product.

Not endorsements. Not party messaging. A practical agenda of technology issues Idaho leaders should understand.

Possible sections:

- broadband accountability
- cybersecurity for counties, schools, clinics, utilities, and small businesses
- water data and measurement infrastructure
- right-to-repair access
- workforce training and technical education
- public-sector software modernization
- AI procurement and privacy rules
- semiconductor and energy infrastructure planning
- rural telehealth and emergency communications
- data-center power and water transparency

This should eventually become an annual report.

### 7. Idaho Implementation Index

A business-facing maturity model.

This is where The Idaho Review becomes invaluable.

The index should let a business ask: **What should we be doing better?**

Categories:

1. Customer intake
2. Scheduling and follow-up
3. Payments and bookkeeping
4. Documentation and records
5. Staff training
6. Cybersecurity basics
7. Data privacy
8. AI readiness
9. Vendor risk
10. Backup and continuity
11. Hardware and repair planning
12. Broadband and outage resilience
13. Website and search presence
14. Workflow automation
15. Measurement and reporting

Each category should have maturity levels:

- Level 0: informal / memory-based
- Level 1: documented but manual
- Level 2: consistent tool use
- Level 3: integrated workflow
- Level 4: measured and improved
- Level 5: resilient, documented, secure, and trainable

This can become a diagnostic, report, consulting product, newsletter series, and public benchmark.

## 12. How We Become Invaluable

The Idaho Review becomes invaluable by doing work that is annoying, specific, and useful enough that others do not do it consistently.

### We maintain maps

Most publications write a story and move on. We maintain the map.

### We track constraints

Most business coverage celebrates announcements. We track what can stop the announcement from becoming reality.

### We translate between groups

Businesses, students, public officials, farmers, engineers, repair shops, and ordinary residents often use different language for related problems. We translate.

### We turn stories into tools

A story about AI adoption becomes a checklist. A story about workforce gaps becomes a skill map. A story about broadband becomes a ground-truth tracker. A story about public software becomes a procurement guide.

### We are useful before we are large

A small site can still be valuable if it answers questions well. The first goal is not volume. The first goal is usefulness dense enough that the right people bookmark it.

Every major page should answer:

1. Who is this for?
2. What decision does it help them make?
3. What Idaho-specific context changes the answer?
4. What sources support it?
5. What should the reader do next?
6. What should we update later?

If a page cannot answer those six questions, it is probably content, not infrastructure.

The Idaho Review should build infrastructure.

## 14. Editorial Independence and Revenue

The Idaho Review can support business opportunities without becoming fake.

The rule:

> Revenue can fund the resource. It cannot decide the truth.

Allowed revenue paths:

- clearly labeled sponsorships
- directory listings with transparent labeling
- job posts
- events
- paid reports
- member briefings
- consulting leads
- research services
- company profile packages only if editorial standards and labels are clear

Protected lines:

- no hidden advertorial
- no pay-to-rank directory ordering without labels
- no unverifiable claims inserted for paying companies
- no client work disguised as reporting
- no political endorsements disguised as analysis
- no publishing claims we cannot source or clearly label

Trust is more valuable than short-term revenue.

## 19. The Short Manifesto

Technology is not somewhere else.

It is already in Idaho’s farms, clinics, labs, schools, shops, utilities, factories, counties, repairs, homes, and small businesses.

The question is whether Idaho understands it well enough to use it wisely.

The Idaho Review exists to build that understanding.

We will map the companies. We will follow the constraints. We will ask who pays, who maintains it, who gets trained, who is exposed, and what happens when it breaks.

We will help businesses decide what to implement. We will help students decide what to study. We will help public leaders decide what to prioritize. We will help ordinary readers see the systems shaping daily life.

We will not mistake announcements for progress.

We will not confuse hype with usefulness.

We will not write about Idaho as if it were a smaller Silicon Valley.

We will start where the work happens.

That is the job.
