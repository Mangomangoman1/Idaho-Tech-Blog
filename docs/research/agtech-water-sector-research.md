# Idaho Agtech and Water Sector Hub — source-aware research notes

Prepared for The Idaho Stack sector hub. Scope: Idaho-specific agriculture, water, irrigation, food processing, groundwater/Snake River/aquifer issues, university/manufacturing resources, and verifiable technology providers. Avoid generic hype: this sector is anchored in water law, irrigation infrastructure, crop and livestock economics, processing capacity, and practical field/manufacturing tech.

## Executive thesis

Idaho’s agtech and water story is not primarily a generic startup story. It is a water-constrained, processing-heavy production system built around the Snake River Plain, senior/junior water rights, irrigation districts and canal companies, dairies, potatoes, sugar beets, hay, grain, seed, and large-scale food manufacturing.

The hub should help readers understand:
- Where the money is: dairy, cattle, potatoes, hay, wheat, sugar beets, barley, seed, and processed food/beverage sales.
- Where the constraint is: aquifer health, drought variability, conjunctive administration of surface and groundwater, canal delivery, recharge, water measurement, and regulatory certainty.
- Where technology actually enters: irrigation scheduling/ET data, meters/telemetry, canal automation, precision application, variable-rate agronomy, drone services, food-safety/process improvement, water reuse, wastewater/reuse in processing, energy efficiency, and public data tools.
- Who decides: IDWR, IWRB, ISDA, irrigation districts/canal companies/groundwater districts, the Surface Water Coalition, groundwater users, processors, University of Idaho Extension/IWRRI, TechHelp, commodity groups, USDA/NASS and Bureau of Reclamation.

## High-confidence facts and source notes

### Agriculture and economy baseline

- ISDA 2025 agriculture statistics say Idaho has 22,600 farms/ranches, 11.5 million acres in farms/ranches, and 3,167,499 irrigated acres. Source: ISDA/USDA NASS 2024 Annual Statistical Bulletin via ISDA 2025 Ag Statistics PDF: https://agri.idaho.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/2025-Idaho-Ag-Statistics-002.pdf
- ISDA reports 2023 agriculture cash receipts of $10.878B, with $6.350B livestock and $4.529B crops. Same source.
- ISDA ranks Idaho nationally in 2023: #1 potatoes, barley, alfalfa hay, peppermint oil, trout; #2 sugar beets and hops; #3 milk, cheese, milk cows; #4 dry onions and spring wheat; #5 all wheat, dry peas, haylage. Same source.
- ISDA lists major 2023 commodity cash receipts: milk $3.460B; cattle/calves $2.605B; potatoes $1.337B; hay $614M; all wheat $608M; sugar beets $465M; barley $422M; corn grain $143M; eggs $120M; onions $110M; hops $91M. Same source.
- ISDA says agriculture plus food and beverage processing is over $26B and generates 17% of Idaho sales and 12.8% of GDP based on 2023 IMPLAN data. Sources: https://agri.idaho.gov/wp-content/uploads/marketing/Publications%26Resources/2025-Ag-Facts-Sheet.pdf and https://agri.idaho.gov/wp-content/uploads/2025/05/2025-Idaho-Ag-Statistics-002.pdf

### Water and governance baseline

- Idaho Department of Water Resources is the core state water agency for water rights, water data, wells, water districts, measurement, legal actions, water supply bank, groundwater management, and maps. URLs: https://idwr.idaho.gov/ ; https://idwr.idaho.gov/water-rights/ ; https://idwr.idaho.gov/water-data/
- The Eastern Snake Plain Aquifer Ground Water Management Area was designated in November 2016. IDWR says an advisory committee was formed to prepare a draft management plan because of ongoing concerns with groundwater diversions. URL: https://idwr.idaho.gov/water-rights/groundwater-management-areas/eastern-snake-plain-aquifer-advisory-committee/
- IDWR/IWRB maintain the Eastern Snake River Plain Aquifer Comprehensive Aquifer Management Plan page. URL: https://idwr.idaho.gov/IWRB/water-planning/CAMPs/ESPA/
- IDWR’s Eastern Snake Plain Aquifer Model page describes the Eastern Snake Hydrologic Modeling Committee, formed in 1998, with state/federal/private/university hydrologists. Its mission includes improving groundwater-flow modeling, representing surface-groundwater interaction, and estimating uncertainty. URL: https://idwr.idaho.gov/water-data/projects/espam/
- ET-IDWR provides evapotranspiration, net irrigation requirement, and effective precipitation estimates for Idaho, with daily/monthly/annual timesteps for 212 weather-station locations. It is intended for irrigation design/management, water-rights management/transfers, and hydrologic studies. URL: https://et-idwr.idaho.gov/
- Idaho Water Resources Research Institute conducts and directs water research, shares information, supports water workforce development, and provides data/tools. URL: https://iwrri.uidaho.edu/
- Idaho Water Users Association provides advocacy, training, member resources, and a member directory for water user entities. URLs: https://iwua.org/ and https://iwua.org/IWUA-Members
- IDWR has an irrigation organizations map that is useful for “who delivers water where.” URL: https://idwr.idaho.gov/water-rights/irrigation-organizations/map/

### Current water conflict and water-certainty theme

- IDWR’s 2024 Surface Water Coalition Delivery Call page says the SWC was formed in 2005 by canal companies and irrigation districts that diverted and delivered Snake River water to about 550,000 acres of farmland in the Eastern Snake Plain area. URL: https://idwr.idaho.gov/legal-actions/delivery-call-actions/swc/2024-surface-water-coalition-delivery-call/
- IDWR’s June 20, 2024 news release says SWC and ESPA groundwater districts reached a settlement for the 2024 irrigation season to avoid curtailment. ESPA groundwater districts would conform to the 2016 mitigation plan, collectively conserve 240,000 acre-feet of groundwater and deliver 50,000 acre-feet of storage water to SWC; parties also committed to negotiate a new mitigation plan. It notes the May 30 curtailment order affected groundwater rights junior to March 31, 1954 unless operating under approved mitigation plans. URL: https://idwr.idaho.gov/wp-content/uploads/sites/2/news-release/20240620-Settlement-reached-between-SWC-and-groundwater-users-FINAL-6.20.24.pdf
- Governor Little’s July 18, 2024 release says the ESPA GWMA Advisory Committee was tasked with a groundwater management plan and implementation work for the 2025 irrigation season. Check latest IDWR implementation status before publishing. URL: https://gov.idaho.gov/pressrelease/gov-little-applauds-groundwater-management-plan-progress/

## Decision framework ideas

Evaluate ag-water technology against:
- Water-right fit: effects on consumptive use, diversion timing, place of use, transfers, mitigation, measurement.
- Acre-foot economics: cost per acre-foot saved, shifted, recharged, or documented; distinguish diversion reduction from consumptive-use reduction.
- Crop/process economics: yield, quality, inputs, labor, contracts, downtime, food safety, energy, wastewater.
- Data defensibility: calibration, auditability, usefulness for water accounting or regulatory review.
- Interoperability: pivots, pumps, meters, SCADA, farm-management systems, processor QA systems, district accounting.
- Labor reality: installation, maintenance, troubleshooting, interpretation during peak seasons.
- Local support: Idaho/Magic Valley/Eastern Idaho service capacity and parts availability.
- Water-energy nexus: pumping energy, pressure, VFDs, pivots, canal lifts, reuse systems, refrigeration/process heat.
- Risk allocation: who benefits and who pays — grower, landlord, canal company, groundwater district, processor, city, taxpayer.
- Policy durability: drought, curtailment risk, mitigation-plan changes, commodity price swings, contract changes.

## Audience playbooks

### Growers, dairies, processors
- Start with water-right and delivery context: surface vs groundwater, district/canal company, mitigation obligations, measurement rules.
- Use ET-IDWR, district data, and Extension resources before buying software.
- Prioritize direct operating value: scheduling, pump energy, variable-rate fertility, yield/quality tracking, labor reduction, compliance documentation.
- Ask vendors whether they document consumptive-use changes or only diversions/soil moisture.
- For dairies/feedlots/processors, track water, nutrients, wastewater/reuse, food safety, energy, refrigeration, and supply contracts together.

### Policymakers and civic leaders
- Treat water certainty as economic development infrastructure.
- Separate “new water” claims from conservation, recharge, storage, reuse, and timing shifts.
- Fund public data infrastructure: ET, meters, groundwater levels, snowpack/runoff, canal measurement, water-right maps.
- Support workforce: ditchriders, pump technicians, food-safety specialists, water lawyers, hydrologists, PLC/SCADA techs, dairy/process engineers.

### Students and workforce entrants
- Career lanes: irrigation technician, agronomist/crop advisor, dairy tech, food safety/QA, maintenance mechanic, controls/automation tech, hydrology/GIS, water law/admin, wastewater/reuse, data analyst.
- Practical stack: pumps/pivots, soil-water sensors, ET, GIS, Python/R/Excel, PLC/SCADA basics, food safety systems, statistics, Spanish language skills.
- Start with UI Extension, UI CALS/IWRRI, College of Southern Idaho, CTE programs, TechHelp courses, internships with districts/processors.

### Residents and voters
- Idaho food production depends on engineered water delivery and legal priority systems, not just rainfall.
- “Conservation” can mean less diversion, less consumptive use, higher efficiency, recharge, reuse, or timing changes.
- Snake River/ESPA connects farms, springs, cities, processors, fish/wildlife, hydropower, recreation, and tribal/regional interests.

### Reporters and editors
- Never publish “acre-feet saved” without asking whether it is diversion, consumptive use, recharge, storage delivery, or model-estimated mitigation credit.
- Map senior/junior rights, district membership, mitigation plan status, and affected crop/processor contracts.
- For expansions, verify water supply, wastewater, milk/potato/beet supply, workforce, energy, traffic, and incentives.
- For vendors, require Idaho customer evidence or in-state operations before calling them “Idaho agtech.”

## Open reporting questions

1. What is the latest adopted/implemented ESPA Groundwater Management Plan status after the 2024 deadlines?
2. Which groundwater districts/canal companies are party to the current mitigation plan, and what users remain outside approved plans?
3. How much aquifer recharge was completed by year over the last decade, by location/canal partner, and how much is recurring vs opportunistic high-flow recharge?
4. Which irrigation districts/canal companies are deploying telemetry, automated gates, SCADA, seepage reduction, or measurement upgrades?
5. What share of Idaho irrigated acres use pivots, drip/micro, flood/furrow, and sprinkler systems, and how has that changed?
6. How do processor contracts influence water-use decisions for potatoes, sugar beets, dairy feed, barley, and seed crops?
7. What are the biggest water-quality issues tied to agriculture/processing by region: nitrate, phosphorus, temperature, wastewater, CAFO/manure nutrients?
8. How are dairies and dairy processors adapting to water, manure, methane, and energy constraints?
9. What local vendors have significant Idaho customer bases for soil moisture sensors, telemetry, variable rate, drone spraying, irrigation design, and food-plant automation?
10. How many food processors have water-reuse/wastewater-reduction systems comparable to Simplot Caldwell?
11. What workforce gaps are processors and irrigation districts reporting?
12. How are cities and subdivisions affecting irrigation organizations, delivery canals, easements, and pressure irrigation?
13. What role should managed aquifer recharge, storage, demand reduction, crop switching, and transfers play in future Snake River water certainty?
14. How do tribal water rights and fisheries interests intersect with Snake River Plain irrigation and recharge policy? Needs dedicated reporting.
15. What can Idaho learn from neighboring basins without importing mismatched policy?

## Claims to avoid or label carefully

- Do not call Idaho an “agtech startup hub” without sourced company count and funding/job data.
- Do not imply irrigation efficiency automatically increases river/aquifer water; distinguish consumptive use, return flows, timing, and legal accounting.
- Do not publish Chobani expansion numbers, Lamb Weston Idaho facility counts, or specific canal-company acreages without primary/current sources.
- Do not frame all ag water conflict as farmers vs cities; ESPA/Snake River issues are often senior surface-water users vs junior groundwater users, plus processors/cities/environmental/tribal/federal interests.
- Do not use statewide averages for individual basins/crops; Idaho’s water, crop, and processing systems are region-specific.
