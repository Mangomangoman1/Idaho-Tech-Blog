## 1) Sector thesis

Idaho repair is not one industry. It is an overlapping set of local services, workforce pipelines, environmental systems, and policy fights:

- Consumer device/electronics repair: phones, laptops, tablets, game consoles, soldering/microsoldering, data recovery, business IT maintenance.
- Farm and heavy equipment maintenance: diesel, hydraulics, electronics, telematics, diagnostic software, parts logistics, dealer service capacity, independent mechanics.
- Industrial and business technology maintenance: POS systems, network gear, printers, surveillance, rugged tablets, fleet devices, backup power, HVAC/control electronics.
- Reuse/e-waste: keeping electronics in service, refurbishing, parts harvesting, certified recycling, county landfill diversion, data destruction.
- Skills and education: diesel/heavy equipment technicians, electronics/mechatronics skills, IT support, soldering, troubleshooting, safety, customer documentation.
- Policy: right-to-repair, warranty law, software locks, emissions-system diagnostics, security pairing, manufacturer parts access, repair documentation.

The Idaho Stack can own this sector by connecting local decision support, policy explainers, and business risk guidance.

## 2) Source-aware findings

### A. Idaho right-to-repair legislation exists in the record but did not become law

- Idaho House Bill 452 in 2020, titled Right to Repair, was introduced by the House Environment, Energy and Technology Committee. The official legislative page says it would add law establishing the Idaho Right to Repair Act. Status shown: introduced 2020-02-07, referred to Environment, Energy and Technology, reported out with recommendation to place on General Orders, then retained on General Orders several times through 2020-03-18. No passage shown on the official status page.
  - Official bill page: https://legislature.idaho.gov/sessioninfo/2020/legislation/H0452/
  - Bill PDF: https://legislature.idaho.gov/wp-content/uploads/sessioninfo/2020/legislation/H0452.pdf
  - Statement of Purpose/Fiscal Note: https://legislature.idaho.gov/wp-content/uploads/sessioninfo/2020/legislation/H0452SOP.pdf
- The bill text proposed an Idaho Right to Repair Act in Title 48 and covered digital electronic equipment, requiring OEMs to make diagnostic/repair documentation, service parts, tools, and firmware available to owners and independent repair providers on fair and reasonable terms. It included exemptions or limitations for trade secrets, security-related equipment, and certain motor vehicle/truck memorandum-of-understanding arrangements.
- Statement of Purpose said OEMs would provide consumers and independent repair businesses equal access to repair documentation, diagnostics, tools, service parts, and firmware as direct/authorized repair providers; it said the bill did not affect retailers, product warranties, or manufacturer liability, and preserved copyright, patent, and trade-secret protections.
- Opposition framing: Idaho Freedom Foundation analysis described HB452 as mandates/regulation on OEMs and rated it negatively. Use as a public-policy counterpoint, not neutral legal authority.
  - https://idahofreedom.org/house-bill-452-right-to-repair-act/

Editorial use: Build a What Idaho has tried timeline. Avoid saying Idaho has a state right-to-repair law unless independently verified later; current research found only an introduced/retained 2020 bill.

### B. Federal repair policy gives Idaho context even without a state law

- FTC Nixing the Fix report, 2021: report to Congress examined repair restrictions and arguments around timing, price, environmental harm, small business, and employment. It documents concerns such as adhesives, limited parts/tools, and unavailable diagnostic software.
  - https://www.ftc.gov/system/files/documents/reports/nixing-fix-ftc-report-congress-repair-restrictions/nixing_the_fix_report_final_5521_630pm-508_002.pdf
- FTC enforcement policy, 2021: FTC announced it would ramp up enforcement against illegal repair restrictions, citing manufacturer methods such as adhesives, parts/tool limitations, and diagnostic-software limits. FTC noted repair restrictions can raise consumer costs, stifle innovation, close independent-shop opportunities, create unnecessary e-waste, and delay timely repairs.
  - https://www.ftc.gov/news-events/news/press-releases/2021/07/ftc-ramp-law-enforcement-against-illegal-repair-restrictions
- Warranty law: FTC Businessperson’s Guide to Federal Warranty Law explains the Magnuson-Moss Warranty Act and warranty disclosure/tie-in issues. Use for practical consumer/business explainers: a warranty is not automatically void merely because a consumer used an independent repairer; however, warranty disputes are fact-specific and damage caused by improper repair may be excluded.
  - https://www.ftc.gov/business-guidance/resources/businesspersons-guide-federal-warranty-law
- Farm/nonroad diesel equipment: EPA’s 2026 press release says EPA guidance clarified that the Clean Air Act supports lawful repair of farm and other nonroad diesel equipment and that manufacturers cannot use the Clean Air Act to justify limiting access to repair tools/software. It states the guidance does not change the law, weaken emission standards, or reduce compliance obligations, and addresses temporary overrides for the purpose of repair.
  - https://www.epa.gov/newsreleases/epa-advances-farmers-right-repair-their-own-equipment-saving-repair-costs-and

Editorial caution: Federal agency press releases can be partisan/administration-framed. Pair press releases with underlying guidance, statute/regulatory text, and stakeholder interviews.

### C. Idaho e-waste policy appears mostly local/voluntary, with DEQ guidance to counties and residents

- Idaho DEQ Residential Household Wastes page says many county landfills collect TVs, computers, and other electronics separately for recycling and advises residents to contact county solid waste departments. If a county lacks electronics recycling, DEQ suggests contacting local elected officials or organizing a household hazardous waste collection event.
  - https://www.deq.idaho.gov/waste-management-and-remediation/solid-waste/residential-household-wastes/
- Idaho DEQ maintains a broader Recycling, Reuse, and Composting page under solid waste.
  - https://www.deq.idaho.gov/waste-management-and-remediation/solid-waste/recycling-reuse-and-composting/

## 4) Decision framework ideas

### Repair / replace / recycle decision tree

1. Safety first: swollen batteries, smoke/liquid damage, exposed mains voltage, hydraulics, airbags, emissions systems, data-sensitive devices.
2. Data and downtime: Is data backed up? What is downtime cost per hour/day? Is loaner/rental equipment available?
3. Warranty/contract status: manufacturer warranty, extended warranty, insurance, lease, dealer maintenance agreement.
4. Parts availability: OEM, aftermarket, used/refurbished, donor parts, lead time, serialization/pairing risk.
5. Software/diagnostic barrier: calibration, pairing, proprietary diagnostic subscription, emissions reset, cloud unlock, firmware.
6. Skill/tooling required: modular replacement vs microsoldering vs hydraulics/diesel diagnostics vs network/system administration.
7. Total cost: quote, risk, downtime, remaining life, resale value, environmental cost.
8. Disposition: repair, refurbish/sell/donate, parts harvest, certified recycle, landfill only if no safe alternative.

### Farm/heavy equipment downtime framework

- What breaks during planting/harvest vs off-season?
- Does the owner have access to diagnostic codes, service manuals, and calibration tools?
- Which failures are safe/legal for in-field repair and which require certified emissions/hydraulics/electrical work?
- How long is dealer field-service response time in rural areas?
- What spare parts should farms stock locally?
- Does purchase/lease agreement permit independent maintenance without warranty penalty?
- What telematics/software subscriptions are required for diagnostics?

### Business technology maintenance framework

- Inventory laptops, POS, routers, switches, printers, surveillance, tablets, phones, backup power.
- Rank by criticality: revenue, compliance, safety/security, convenience.
- Keep spares: chargers, batteries, SSDs, networking gear, backup laptop/POS terminal.
- Document vendors, warranties, serials, passwords, recovery keys, MDM ownership, backups.
- Choose support model: internal, independent repair shop, MSP, manufacturer depot, hybrid.
- Plan disposal: data wipe/destruction certificate, asset tags, donation/reuse, recycle.

### Consumer device repair checklist

- Is the quote diagnostic-only, fixed price, or estimate range?
- Are parts OEM, aftermarket, used, refurbished? What warranty applies?
- Will water resistance remain after repair? Usually not guaranteed unless tested.
- Will Face ID/Touch ID/True Tone/battery health/camera calibration be affected by parts pairing?
- Is data backed up? Will the shop need passcode access? What is privacy policy?
- What happens if repair attempt fails due to board damage/liquid corrosion?

## 5) Audience playbooks

Residents/families:
- Back up before repair; ask about data privacy.
- Do not puncture swollen lithium batteries; isolate safely and contact qualified recycler/repairer.
- Compare repair cost to replacement, but include data transfer, accessories, e-waste, and learning curve.
- Use county solid waste pages for electronics disposal; call first because accepted items/fees change.
- Consider reuse/donation when devices are functional but outdated.

Small businesses:
- Maintain asset inventory and replacement calendar.
- Standardize devices where possible.
- Negotiate repairability into purchase decisions: service manuals, parts availability, local support, warranties, data ownership.
- Keep documented backups and admin credentials separate from one employee/vendor.
- For POS/network gear, keep a tested spare and printed outage procedure.

Farmers/ranchers:
- Before buying equipment, ask who can access diagnostics, what subscriptions are required, what calibrations are dealer-only, and what emissions repairs are allowed.
- Track downtime costs by machine and season.
- Train one employee on preventive maintenance, codes, safe lockout/tagout, hydraulics, and documentation.
- Stock high-failure parts and consumables before planting/harvest.
- Keep repair records to protect warranty and resale value.

Repair shops/technicians:
- Publish warranty, data, and parts policies.
- Track failure modes and anonymized repair stats.
- Invest in documentation, ESD safety, battery handling, customer communication, and quote discipline.
- Build referral relationships: device repair, data recovery, board repair, e-waste, MSP, school programs.
- Avoid overclaiming authorized status; distinguish authorized, independent, OEM parts, and aftermarket parts.

Students/career switchers:
- Diesel/heavy equipment programs have clear Idaho pathways at CEI, CSI, NIC, ISU.
- Electronics/device repair may require apprenticeship, self-study, maker spaces, surplus labs, vendor training, or IT/electronics programs.
- Transferable skills: troubleshooting, schematics/manuals, safe disassembly, measurement tools, soldering, customer documentation, software/firmware, business ethics.

Policymakers/local governments:
- Identify repair deserts by county.
- Measure e-waste collection access and costs.
- Consult independent repairers and OEM/dealer/service networks.
- Focus on practical access: documentation, parts, diagnostic tools, fair pricing, cybersecurity, emissions compliance, liability.
- Support workforce programs and county collection infrastructure before/alongside legislation.
