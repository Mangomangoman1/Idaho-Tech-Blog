# Idaho Energy and Nuclear sector hub research notes

Date: 2026-05-19. Scope: source-aware desk notes for The Idaho Stack; emphasize official/public URLs and decision support. Caveat: web_search/web_extract credits failed; notes were grounded with direct public pages/PDFs fetched via terminal, plus a few official INL/NRIC URLs that were reachable only as 403/Cloudflare from this environment and should be manually checked before publication.

## Executive takeaways

- Idaho is a reliability, nuclear R&D, hydropower, transmission, and large-load planning state. Treat energy as an economic-development constraint and public-policy tradeoff, not generic clean-tech hype.
- INL is the anchor: DOE nuclear energy R&D, advanced reactor demonstration support, microreactor experiments, critical infrastructure/cybersecurity, integrated energy systems, workforce, and regional economic impact.
- Idaho Power 2025 IRP says peak load grows about 1,700 MW over 20 years, with nearly 1,000 MW in the next five years. Its 20-year plan adds 1,445 MW solar, 885 MW battery storage, 700 MW wind, 611 MW coal-to-gas conversion, 550 MW new gas, 344 MW energy efficiency, and 20 MW incremental demand response, plus major transmission assumptions.
- Large loads are now a front-page planning variable. Idaho Power models additional large-load cases of 300 MW and 500 MW. OEMR says Western demand pressure is coming from data centers, manufacturing, cryptocurrency, and electrification. Exact Idaho data center commitments need reporting, not assumption.
- Nuclear enthusiasm should be bounded. Idaho has unmatched test infrastructure and talent, but commercial deployment is still technology-, licensing-, cost-, supply-chain-, customer-, and community-dependent. UAMPS/NuScale CFPP cancellation is a key reality check.
- Storage should distinguish bankable lithium-ion BESS from long-duration options. Official sources reviewed mention compressed air as a technology class, but I found no current named Idaho Power CAES procurement or named Idaho CAES project.

## Key entities

- INL: DOE lab near Idaho Falls; nuclear energy R&D, advanced reactor demonstrations, cybersecurity/critical infrastructure, integrated energy, materials, national security. OEMR 2026 Landscape says annual research budget exceeds $2B and economic contribution is nearly $4B; verify current INL economic impact before publishing.
- DOE Office of Nuclear Energy: federal nuclear R&D and advanced reactor programs relevant to INL.
- NRIC: public-private bridge for advanced reactor testing; relevant to DOME/test beds and developer access to INL capability.
- OEMR: state energy policy clearinghouse; resilience and efficiency funding; ISEA and advanced nuclear framework; publications.
- IPUC: utility rates, IRP acknowledgment, CPCNs, tariffs, customer generation, cost allocation.
- Idaho Power/IDACORP: largest electric utility; hydropower-heavy legacy portfolio; IRP/RFP/transmission leader.
- Avista and Rocky Mountain Power/PacifiCorp: major IOUs in northern/southeastern Idaho.
- BPA, coops, and munis: vital for public-power Idaho; use OEMR utility contact list for current roster.
- Nuclear private-sector tracker: NuScale/UAMPS as cautionary canceled project; Project Pele, MARVEL, MCRE, NRIC/DOME as demo/testing topics; do not list other developers as Idaho projects unless current official agreements are confirmed.

## Idaho Power/grid notes

- IRP hub text fetched says Idaho Power submitted 2025 IRP June 27, 2025; Oregon PUC acknowledged in December; Idaho PUC acknowledged in February. Verify order numbers.
- Demand: peak load projected to grow about 1,700 MW over 20 years, nearly 1,000 MW in five years. Customer count expected to rise from nearly 648,000 in Dec. 2024 to 867,000 in 2045.
- Planned additions: 1,445 MW solar; 885 MW battery storage; 700 MW wind; 611 MW converted coal-to-gas; 550 MW new gas; 344 MW energy efficiency; 20 MW incremental demand response.
- Transmission: B2H 500-kV line modeled for Dec. 2027; SWIP-N 500-kV Idaho-Nevada/Las Vegas-area connectivity in 2028; Midpoint-Hemingway #2/Gateway West segment 8 phases in 2028 and 2030.
- Large-load uncertainty: IRP studies 300 MW and 500 MW additional large-load scenarios and emphasizes flexibility around customer growth and federal/state policy.
- Existing assets mentioned in extraction: 17 hydropower projects; Jim Bridger coal/natural gas; North Valmy conversion to gas; Langley Gulch CCCT; Bennett Mountain/Danskin SCCTs; Hemingway, Black Mesa, Franklin, Distributed, and Happy Valley BESS assets.
- Storage: IRP models lithium-ion batteries, longer-duration iron-air-type storage, and pumped hydro; mentions compressed air as a technology class but not as a selected near-term resource.

## Nuclear and advanced reactors

- Segment INL coverage into research reactors/facilities, private test beds, defense microreactors, commercial grid prospects, cyber/critical infrastructure, workforce, and supplier base.
- OEMR 2026 Landscape says INL will host first-of-a-kind reactor demonstrations and names MARVEL, Project Pele, and MCRE.
- MARVEL: OEMR describes a microreactor producing up to 20 kW, enough to power 10 homes. It is a research/demo reactor, not grid supply.
- NRIC/DOME: explain as enabling environment/test bed, not a guarantee of commercial deployment.
- CFPP/NuScale: use as case study on offtake/subscriber risk, cost escalation, licensing timeline, and municipal utility risk tolerance.
- Key public questions: spent fuel, HALEU/fuel supply, NRC vs DOE authority, emergency planning, tribal/state/local roles, decommissioning.

## Cybersecurity and critical infrastructure

- Boise State’s Institute for Pervasive Cybersecurity page says it leads cybersecurity research and advancement in Idaho/region, partners with industry/higher ed/business/government, commercializes tools, and educates graduates.
- INL Cybercore should be framed as national security/critical infrastructure asset: grid OT security, industrial control systems, nuclear cyber, telecom/resilience, threat-informed engineering. Verify current claims on INL pages before publication.
- Reporting angle: rural coops and munis face cyber needs with fewer staff than IOUs; ask about federal grants, mutual aid, tabletop exercises, incident disclosure, supply-chain risk, OT/IT segmentation.

## Data centers and large-load demand

- OEMR 2026 Landscape says Western electricity demand is projected to increase 20 percent over the next decade, mostly due to data centers, manufacturing, cryptocurrency, and electrification.
- Idaho Power models 300 MW and 500 MW additional large-load scenarios and says industrial interest is significant.
- I found no official single list of Idaho data center projects/queues in the reviewed documents. Reporting should use county land-use filings, utility interconnection queues, IPUC dockets, tax incentive agreements, and economic-development announcements.

- OEMR Landscape lists compressed air among common mechanical storage technologies.
- Idaho Power IRP mentions compressed air, flywheels, pumped hydro, iron-air, etc.; selected/modeling emphasis is Li-ion BESS, longer-duration iron-air-type batteries, and pumped hydro.
- Open reporting path: INL has public-news references to Idaho researchers looking underground for energy storage solutions; verify exact current article/project.

## Decision frameworks

### Resource scorecard
- Reliability contribution: firm capacity, ELCC, winter/summer performance, black-start/grid services.
- Cost/rate impact: capital, fuel, O&M, transmission upgrades, tax credits, risk allocation.
- Deliverability: transmission path, interconnection queue, permitting, construction timeline.
- Technology maturity: commercial operating history vs first-of-a-kind/demo.
- Policy/regulatory fit: IPUC/Oregon PUC, federal rules, emissions, PURPA/CSPP, local zoning.
- Community impact: land, water, wildfire, wildlife, cultural resources, tax base, jobs, housing.
- Optionality: can it scale, pause, repower, or adapt to load uncertainty?

### Large-load approval framework
- Signed contract or speculative inquiry?
- Deposits/collateral/minimum demand/exit fees?
- Who pays network upgrades and stranded costs?
- Interruptible/curtailable provisions or onsite generation/storage?
- Water, land, emergency services, property tax, and workforce impacts?
- What can be public without compromising customer confidentiality?

### Advanced nuclear readiness framework
- Use case: research/demo, remote power, industrial heat, data center PPA, grid capacity, defense.
- Licensing path: DOE authorization vs NRC license; site control; emergency planning.
- Fuel: HALEU availability, fabrication, transport, security.
- Offtake: buyer, price, tenor, and risk allocation.
- Waste/decommissioning: responsibilities and funding.
- Local benefits/risks: jobs, suppliers, tax base, emergency services, community consent.

### Cyber/grid resilience framework
- Critical loads: hospital, water, telecom, emergency services.
- Threat model: wildfire, extreme cold/heat, cyber/OT compromise, supply chain, physical security.
- Redundancy: feeder diversity, backup generation, microgrids, storage, mutual aid.
- Governance: incident reporting, exercises, board oversight, insurance.
- Funding: FEMA/DOE/state resilience grants, utility rate recovery.

## Audience playbooks

### Business/site selectors
- Start with service territory and substation/transmission proximity; ask for available capacity and timeline in writing.
- Identify ordinary commercial vs large-load vs interruptible vs special contract.
- Compare delivered energy cost, capacity cost, interconnection cost, and reliability, not headline cents/kWh.
- For clean-power claims, require matched deliverable products and understand REC/accounting rules.
- Watch IPUC dockets and Idaho Power RFP/IRP updates.

### Policymakers/local governments
- Separate announcements from binding load commitments.
- Require cost-causation analysis for large loads; protect existing customers from stranded costs.
- Coordinate zoning, water, emergency response, and tax decisions with utility planning.
- Treat nuclear demos as high-skill economic development but ask about schedule, offtake, waste, and emergency planning.
- Support workforce pipelines for lineworkers, electricians, welders, nuclear operators, cyber/OT engineers, and permitting staff.

### Students/workers
- Paths: nuclear/mechanical/electrical engineering, cybersecurity/OT, power systems, line work, instrumentation and controls, welding, radiation protection, project management, environmental permitting.
- Institutions to investigate: UI, ISU, Boise State, CEI, BYU-Idaho, Idaho WDC, Northwest Lineman College, apprenticeships, INL internships.
- Durable skills: NERC/grid operations, SCADA/ICS security, high-voltage safety, Python/data analytics, NRC/DOE safety culture, technical writing.

### Residents/ratepayers
- Ask: Will bills rise? Will reliability improve? Who bears risk if forecasts are wrong?
- IRPs are plans, not construction approvals; CPCN/rate cases/RFPs are where commitments harden.
- Hydropower helps costs/carbon, but drought, fish constraints, growth, and regional markets matter.
- Large loads may help tax base/jobs but can require expensive grid investments.

### Reporters/editors
- Build recurring trackers: IPUC dockets; IRP/RFP milestones; INL/NRIC demos; large-load projects; transmission permits; wildfire/resilience plans; workforce grants.
- Use labels: confirmed, proposed, studied, speculative.
- Avoid “powers X homes” claims without capacity factor and deliverability context.
