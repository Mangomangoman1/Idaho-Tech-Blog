# Idaho Broadband and Infrastructure Sector Hub — source-aware research notes

Purpose: working research for The Idaho Review sector hub. Treat as a living source map, not a finished article. Where a source could not be directly fetched because of Cloudflare or payment restrictions, the URL is still listed and uncertainty is flagged.

## 1) Sector thesis for Idaho

Idaho broadband policy is now a public-infrastructure accountability story, not just a telecom story. The state is trying to connect high-cost rural, mountain, tribal, and agricultural areas while avoiding duplicate public subsidies and verifying whether FCC map data matches lived service conditions. The core reader need is decision support: who pays, who builds, who is still left out, which public institutions gain resilience, and whether businesses and residents can rely on service during outages, fire season, winter travel interruptions, remote work, telehealth, and school operations.

## 3) Core verified facts and notes

### Governance
- The Idaho Office of Broadband, IOB, is the BEAD eligible entity named in Idaho’s Initial Proposal documents.
- Idaho Broadband Advisory Board, IBAB, was created by the Idaho Legislature in 2021 via House Bill 127. The BEAD Volume II text says IBAB consists of three Idaho House members, three Idaho Senate members, and three gubernatorial appointees.
- IBAB review and award is part of the proposed BEAD subgrantee selection workflow after compliance/completeness, state administrative review, and committee scoring.

### BEAD funding scale
- Idaho’s BEAD allocation is $583,256,249.88. Source: Idaho BEAD Volume I/II text citing NTIA’s June 26, 2023 allocation announcement.
- Idaho’s BEAD plan prioritizes unserved and underserved broadband serviceable locations, BSLs, then eligible community anchor institutions, CAIs, lacking 1 Gbps symmetrical service.
- Definitions in the BEAD docs: unserved means less than 25/3 Mbps; underserved means lacking reliable broadband service at 100/20 Mbps and latency no more than 100 ms.

### Map and count caution
- Idaho Volume I final text says the IOB identified 92,471 unserved and 63,723 underserved BSLs based on FCC National Broadband Map availability data published Nov. 28, 2023.
- The Volume II redline copy found in research says 85,902 unserved and 52,094 underserved BSLs based on FCC July 12, 2023 and CostQuest December 2022 data.
- Editorial handling: use the newer Volume I final counts only if confirmed against the final approved BEAD documents or current NTIA/FCC data. In public copy, say counts changed with map version and challenge process and link to the current FCC/IOB map rather than treating one number as timeless.

### BEAD selection framework from Idaho Volume II
- Proposed scoring categories highlighted in Volume II:
  - Primary criteria: 75 points / 75 percent total weight: minimal BEAD outlay, 50 points; affordability, 15; fair labor practices, 10.
  - Secondary criteria: speed to deployment, 5; speed/network/technical capabilities, 5; open access network, 7.5; local and Tribal coordination, 5.
- Five reservations are represented as singular application project areas in the described project-area structure. Use care: this is a BEAD application-design detail, not a statement that tribal needs are uniform.
- Idaho intends to set an Extremely High Cost Per Location Threshold, with NTIA guidance pushing fiber wherever feasible while allowing alternative technologies in very high-cost areas.
- Prospective subgrantees must satisfy financial, managerial, technical, legal, operational, labor, and compliance requirements; the plan discusses monitoring practices.

### Capital Projects Fund and earlier public funding
- U.S. Treasury approved Idaho’s plan to invest $120 million of Capital Projects Fund money in broadband infrastructure, described as 93 percent of Idaho’s CPF allocation.
- Treasury fact sheet estimates 35,000 locations served and says the BIG-CPF program targets areas lacking at least 100/20 Mbps, with projects designed for 100/100 Mbps upon completion.
- Treasury fact sheet says funded ISPs would participate in the FCC Affordable Connectivity Program. Update note: ACP’s funding status changed after the fact sheet; any affordability promises should be checked against current subsidy programs and provider low-cost plans.

### Tribal and rural/mountain connectivity themes
- Idaho BEAD Volume II includes consultations or notes involving Coeur d’Alene Tribe, Kootenai Tribe, Nez Perce Tribe, Shoshone-Bannock Tribes, and Shoshone-Paiute Tribes.
- Example issue from Volume II: Shoshone-Paiute notes describe need for redundant fiber pathing, including a desired southern path toward Elko, Nevada, because existing fiber interruptions can interrupt all internet service. This is a strong case study for resilience, not just speed. Verify with tribal officials before publication.
- Mountain/rural Idaho constraints to track: high construction cost per passing, federal/state land permitting, winter construction windows, wildfire/smoke/terrain risk, long backhaul routes, sparse take rates, outdated electronics/switching, and lack of redundant middle-mile routes.

### Schools, clinics, emergency services, anchor institutions
- BEAD CAI policy: eligible CAIs lacking 1 Gbps symmetrical connectivity may be served after unserved/underserved BSL priorities.
- State Board of Education maintains a Broadband Access for Idaho Schools page; connect this hub to E-rate, district network resiliency, cybersecurity, and outage contingency planning.
- Emergency-communications angle: broadband affects 911 centers, public safety answering points, emergency communications centers, wildfire response, search-and-rescue, and hospital/clinic continuity. IOB Volume II acronyms include ECC; confirm which ECC/911 modernization projects are broadband-dependent through Idaho Office of Emergency Management and local emergency communications districts.

## 5) Decision framework ideas for readers

### A. Is this broadband project accountable? checklist
- Need: Which BSLs/CAIs are targeted, and are they unserved/underserved under the current FCC map?
- Duplication: Is the location already subject to CPF, RDOF, ReConnect, state, local, or enforceable private commitments?
- Technology: fiber, fixed wireless, coax upgrade, satellite, hybrid? What speeds, latency, capacity, and upgrade path are promised?
- Resilience: Does the project add redundant routes, backup power, diverse backhaul, wildfire hardening, and winter repair capability?
- Affordability: What is the actual monthly plan after taxes/fees/equipment? Are low-cost options durable after ACP?
- Open access/competition: Does public money create wholesale/open access or just one retail monopoly? If no open access, why?
- Local/Tribal coordination: Were local governments, schools, clinics, libraries, and tribal governments consulted and documented?
- Workforce/permitting: Are labor, Davis-Bacon, Buy America, rights-of-way, pole attachments, and NEPA risks realistic?
- Performance: How will the state verify build completion, service availability, latency, uptime, and customer adoption?

### B. Broadband resilience scorecard for businesses/public agencies
- Primary connection type and SLA.
- Secondary path using different physical route/provider/technology.
- Backup power for modem/router/ONT/firewall and key switches.
- Cellular or LEO satellite failover for payment systems, dispatch, telehealth, POS, security cameras, and remote work.
- DNS/cloud dependency map: which tools fail if the local ISP fails?
- Incident plan: staff contacts, provider escalation, offline workflows, customer communication template.
- Test cadence: quarterly failover tests; annual tabletop for outage plus winter storm/wildfire scenario.

### C. Rural build feasibility explainer
- Cost per passing vs. total public outlay.
- Middle mile vs. last mile distinction.
- Take-rate risk and affordability risk.
- Permitting/ROW ownership and pole attachment delays.
- Terrain, weather, federal lands, tribal lands, and cross-state routing constraints.
- Why map challenges matter before award, not after construction.

## 6) Audience playbooks

### For residents
- Check address on FCC National Broadband Map and provider sites; document actual service with bills/speed tests/outage logs.
- If mapped as served but service is unavailable or slower, file FCC availability/speed challenges and notify county/IOB contacts.
- Ask providers: install cost, data caps, upload speed, latency, contract term, equipment fees, low-income plan, and outage support.

### For small businesses / farms / remote workers
- Do not rely on advertised download speed alone; verify upload, latency, uptime, restoration time, and static IP/VPN needs.
- Budget for failover: second ISP, cellular router, or satellite backup; test payment-processing and cloud-app continuity.
- Track outage cost: lost sales, payroll, spoilage, dispatch failures. This becomes evidence for local broadband advocacy.

### For policymakers / county commissioners / city leaders
- Build a county source file: unserved/underserved BSLs, CAIs, prior awards, provider commitments, ROW bottlenecks, shovel-ready projects.
- Demand award transparency: project areas, public dollars per location, technology, performance milestones, penalties, and post-build verification.
- Coordinate with emergency management, school districts, libraries, clinics, tribes, economic development, and road/utility departments.

### For schools, libraries, clinics
- Map anchor-institution bandwidth, cybersecurity, backup power, and failover needs.
- Separate classroom/telehealth capacity from administrative redundancy; both matter.
- Use E-rate/USAC and BEAD/CPF source streams carefully; avoid duplicate funding for the same scope.

### For tribal governments
- Treat broadband as sovereignty, public safety, education, and economic development infrastructure.
- Preserve ownership/control options: tribal utility, open-access middle mile, IRU agreements, partnership terms, and data governance.
- Document missing FCC Fabric locations and service-quality evidence early; pursue redundant paths where single-route dependence creates outage vulnerability.

### For students / researchers
- Track how map versions change location eligibility.
- Compare promised vs. delivered speeds and affordability.
- Build county case studies: one mountain county, one reservation/community, one growth-corridor city, one agricultural region.

## 10) Publication cautions

- Do not state provider coverage from marketing pages alone; verify with FCC BDC, state awards, local officials, and customer evidence.
- Treat FCC map counts as versioned data. Always include map date/version.
- Avoid closing-the-digital-divide claims unless tied to specific addresses, affordability, and adoption metrics.
- Keep tribal broadband reporting consent-based and source-specific; do not collapse distinct tribes/reservations into one rural category.
- Separate availability from adoption, affordability, reliability, and resilience.
- Cloudflare blocked several official Idaho pages in this research environment; before final publication, manually confirm current URLs, approval status, and downloadable PDFs.
